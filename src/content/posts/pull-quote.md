Typography is, at its foundation, a technology of reading — an accumulation of small decisions about space and proportion that together determine whether the reader notices the type at all, or is simply carried forward by it.

:::pullquote
The line between craft and invisibility is not a destination. It is the work itself, reconstituted sentence by sentence.

— Eleanor Marsh
:::

The paragraph mark — the pilcrow — has survived every typographic revolution since the manuscript era. It was the original paragraph break, inserted by a rubricator in a different ink after the scribe had finished the text. It outlasted the raised dot, the virgule, and the blank line, and it will probably outlast the convention of indentation too.

:::pullquote
There is no neutral typography. Every choice is an argument about what the reader deserves.
:::

Considered editorial typography asks that the eye travel from sentence to sentence without impediment, which is why the *best* typesetting is the kind **readers never remark upon**. A measure that is too wide forces the eye to traverse too much horizontal distance between saccades. A measure too narrow fractures the rhythm of the sentence itself. The <a href="https://practicaltypography.com/line-length.html">classical range</a> of forty-five to seventy-five characters per line was not arrived at arbitrarily; it corresponds to the distance the eye can comfortably travel at reading distance before needing to return.

:::pullquote
*Emphasis* is the exception, not the default. **Strength** is earned by **restraint** everywhere else. A good sentence needs <a href="https://practicaltypography.com/bold-or-italic.html">neither</a> — but when it does, `<em>` and `<strong>` exist to serve it, not to perform.
:::

Extraordinarily long quotations present their own challenges: the eye must maintain attention across more lines than the surrounding body, which is why pull quotes should, as a general convention, be set at a size that compresses their measure enough to keep the line count brief. A twenty-word pull quote at a narrower measure may produce three or four lines — and that is acceptable, provided the line-height allows each line to breathe. Hyphenopoly's syllabification of multisyllabic English words like *characteristically* or *unconstitutional* should break across lines cleanly here, and the orphan guard should catch any resulting short right fragments.

:::pullquote
The representational power of a typesetting system is not only characteristically measured by its handling of familiar scripts — the unconditional assumption that all prose is monolingual has been a representational failure of editorial infrastructure for centuries, one worth addressing fundamentally rather than superficially.

— Chanikul Kongpradit
:::
